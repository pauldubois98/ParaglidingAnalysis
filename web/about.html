Paragliding thermal simulations — How the simulation works

Terrain: SRTM 30 m (sim) + AWS Terrarium ~7 m (mesh)  ·  Land cover: ESA WorldCover 2021  ·  Calibration: ERA5 reanalysis (Open-Meteo)


Visualization tool  ·  Area Selection  ·  Run simulation

Pipeline overview

1Terrain (dual-resolution) — SRTM 30 m DEM for simulation physics; AWS Terrarium ~7 m tiles for the visual mesh
2Land cover — ESA WorldCover 2021 (10 m) resampled to terrain grid, converted to thermal factors
3Sun positions — real astronomical ephemeris (pysolar) every 30 min during daylight
4Solar heating — direct beam + diffuse radiation per cell, with shadow ray-casting
5Thermal inertia — ground temperature accumulates across time steps with a relaxation constant
6Orographic flow — anabatic upslope wind field derived from temperature gradient
7Trigger factor — static map combining terrain convexity (∇²Z) and land-cover boundary gradient; identifies where thermals preferentially release
8Collecting zones — heat-weighted mean flow convergence (−div u,v) accumulated over all scenarios; shows where thermal energy funnels together
9Export — all scenarios + static fields packed into a JSON file served to the Three.js viewer

Effects taken into account

Sun position & geometry

  • Real astronomical ephemeris modelled — pysolar computes exact azimuth and elevation for the center of the selected region at every time step, for any chosen date.
  • Slope-aspect incidence angle modelled — surface normals are derived from the DEM gradient. A south-facing 30° slope receives more direct beam than a flat or north-facing surface. Formula: cos(θ) = n · s (dot product of surface normal and sun direction).
  • DEM horizon shadowing modelled — for every cell, a ray is cast toward the sun. If any terrain point along that ray is higher than the sun ray, the cell is in terrain shadow and receives no direct beam.

Atmosphere

  • Atmospheric transmittance modelled — the beam weakens as it passes through the atmosphere. Modelled with the Kasten-Young air mass formula and the Bird clear-sky model:
    AM = 1 / (sin(el) + 0.50572 × (el_deg + 6.07995)⁻¹·⁶³⁶⁴) T_atm = 0.7 ^ (AM ^ 0.678) At el=68° (midday): T_atm ≈ 0.70 At el=14° (dawn): T_atm ≈ 0.38
    A low sun delivers much less energy than geometry alone would suggest.
  • Diffuse sky radiation modelled — cells in terrain shadow still receive diffuse radiation from the sky dome. Modelled using an isotropic sky assumption:
    I_diffuse = 0.3 × (1 − T_atm) × SVF SVF = (1 + cos(slope)) / 2 ← sky view factor
    More scattering at low sun angles → higher diffuse fraction at dawn and dusk. Flat plateaux see more sky than deep valley floors (SVF closer to 1).

Ground heating

  • Land-cover thermal factor modelled — different surfaces heat at different rates depending on albedo and thermal inertia. Based on ESA WorldCover 2021 classes:
    SurfaceThermal factorτ (h)Reason
    Bare rock / scree1.800.5Low albedo, negligible heat capacity — spikes at noon
    Built-up1.300.75Concrete / tarmac
    Grassland1.001.5Reference
    Cropland0.851.3Moderate inertia
    Shrubland0.702.0Partial canopy
    Tree cover0.453.75High inertia, canopy evaporation — peaks in early afternoon
    Permanent water0.0322.5Very high heat capacity — nearly constant temperature
  • Thermal inertia modelled — the ground does not instantly reach its equilibrium temperature. Heat accumulates over the day using a first-order low-pass filter:
    dT_carry(t) = dT_carry(t−1) × decay + dT_instant × (1 − decay) decay = exp(−dt / τ), τ = 1.5 h
    This causes thermals to lag behind the sun, peaking in the early afternoon rather than at solar noon.
  • Absolute calibration from ERA5 modelled — the scale factor max_heat is derived automatically from ERA5 reanalysis data (Open-Meteo) for the specific simulation date. Clear-sky midday values of surface_temperature − temperature_2m from the past 5 years are used to set the amplitude. This implicitly captures seasonal effects (soil moisture, vegetation state) without explicit modelling.

Thermal structure

  • Thermal top height modelled — the ceiling where a thermal parcel loses buoyancy, derived from the ground temperature anomaly and atmospheric lapse rates:
    h_top = dT / (DALR − ELR) DALR = 9.8 K/km (fixed by thermodynamics) ELR = adjustable via slider (standard: 6.5 K/km)
  • Collector and trigger zones modelled — a collector is a broad area that accumulates heat and where thermally-driven flow converges. A trigger is the specific terrain or boundary feature where that heat detaches as a thermal.
    • Collecting zones map — for each scenario step the flow divergence div(u,v) is computed from the orographic field; negative divergence (convergence) is weighted by the local temperature anomaly and accumulated across all scenarios. The result is a static heat-weighted mean convergence field, normalised to [0, 1]. Cells that are both warm and in a convergent part of the flow score highest. The map responds to the trigger-threshold slider (same as Daily heat).
    • Trigger zones map — combines terrain convexity (negated Laplacian −∇²Z — ridges and peaks have ∇²Z < 0) with land-cover boundary gradient (abrupt surface transitions) into a single static score, blended 60/40. Bright spots on the trigger map at warm locations on the heat or collector map identify the best thermal sources.
  • Thermal release cooling modelled — cells with a high trigger factor lose heat faster once their temperature anomaly exceeds a release threshold (default 1.5 K). Each time step a fraction of the excess is removed: ΔT ← ΔT − trigger_factor × max(0, ΔT − T_release) × K × dt, with K = 0.3 h⁻¹. This simulates the ground cooling after a thermal detaches. The effect is strongest on ridge crests and land-cover boundaries where trigger_factor peaks, creating realistic warm/cool patterning that propagates through subsequent time steps.
  • Heat advection along orographic flow modelled — after the release step each time step, the temperature anomaly field is advected along the orographic flow field using a first-order upwind finite-difference scheme. The peak advection speed is computed dynamically each time step from terrain steepness and current heating, following the anabatic-wind scaling:
    V_advect = V_max × √( clip(slope_75th / slope_ref) × clip(mean_dT / max_heat) ) V_max = 1 000 m/h — calibrated ceiling slope_ref = 0.30 — characteristic slope (tan 17°) slope_75th = 75th-percentile terrain gradient (avoids flat-valley bias) mean_dT = mean temperature anomaly over heated cells V_advect ∈ [50, 1 000] m/h
    This means advection is nearly idle at dawn (weak heating, slow anabatic flow) and peaks around solar noon (strong heating + steep slopes drive fast upslope air). The time step is subdivided adaptively to keep the CFL number ≤ 0.9. The practical effect is that collector zones (convergent areas) accumulate warmth advected from surrounding slopes, and trigger zones at their edges see enhanced temperature gradients — the "collector steals from upslope, trigger steals from collector" cycle. The dynamic speed also means the collector map naturally weights midday scenarios more heavily, matching the period when paragliding thermals are active.
  • Orographic / anabatic flow modelled — warm air tends to rise along slopes (anabatic wind). The flow direction is the terrain gradient weighted by local temperature anomaly, then Gaussian-smoothed. Cross-valley components cancel, leaving valley-channelled flow.
  • Thermal plume profile and vertical speed estimate estimated — clicking any cell shows a side-panel plume profile. The width of the plume at each altitude encodes the estimated vertical speed at that height, derived from simple parcel buoyancy theory (no entrainment, no turbulence):
    w(z) = √(2 · g/T₀ · ΔT(z) · zAGL)
    where ΔT(z) = ΔTeff · (1 − z/H) (linear temperature excess decaying to zero at the ceiling), g = 9.81 m/s², T₀ = 293 K, and H is the thermal top height. Normalised by its maximum, this gives a width profile w(z)/wmax = 2·√(z/H · (1 − z/H)), which peaks at mid-height (z = H/2). The peak speed label shown in the panel is:
    wmax = √(0.5 · g/T₀ · ΔTeff · H)
    This is a rough order-of-magnitude estimate only. Real thermals are slower than this formula predicts because it ignores entrainment of surrounding air (which dilutes buoyancy and dissipates kinetic energy), turbulent drag, and the fact that a thermal is not a closed parcel but a continuous plume. Typical real values for paragliding thermals are 1–4 m/s; the formula may overestimate by a factor of 2–3 in strong conditions. Use it as a relative indicator (stronger ΔT and higher ceiling → faster thermal) rather than an absolute number.
  • Trigger threshold modelled — a user-adjustable minimum temperature anomaly (in K) used as a display filter, not a physical barrier. The simulation computes ground temperature anomalies independently of this value; the threshold is applied afterwards in the browser as max(0, ΔT − threshold) when colouring the Thermal heat, Thermal top, Daily heat, and Collecting zones maps. Raising it masks out weakly heated cells and shows only the strongest sources — it does not cause heat to accumulate and release at a higher level. Think of it as a confidence cutoff: "only show zones warmer than X K above the local mean".

Simplifications made

  • No fluid dynamics simplified — the model is purely diagnostic. It computes where and when the ground heats up, but does not solve the Navier-Stokes equations. There is no pressure field, no mass conservation, and no atmospheric convection feedback. The orographic flow is a proxy, not a CFD result.
  • Always clear sky simplified — cloud cover is not modelled. The simulation represents a theoretical ideal clear-sky day for the chosen date. Real days will be affected by cumulus, morning fog, etc.
  • Fixed ELR simplified — the Environmental Lapse Rate is a single value for the whole domain and the whole day. In reality it varies with altitude, time, and atmospheric sounding. The slider allows manual adjustment.
  • Thermal inertia by land-cover type modelled — each cell has its own relaxation time constant τ derived from its ESA WorldCover class. Bare rock (τ ≈ 0.5 h) heats and cools rapidly, peaking sharply at solar noon. Dense forest (τ ≈ 3.75 h) responds slowly and stays warm well into the afternoon. Water (τ ≈ 22 h) barely changes temperature within a single day. Grassland (τ = 1.5 h) is the reference. The per-cell decay factor is applied at every time step: dT(t) = dT(t−1) × exp(−dt/τ) + dT_instant × (1 − exp(−dt/τ)). The τ values are estimates — soil moisture, vegetation density, and slope aspect all affect the true value.
  • Isotropic diffuse sky simplified — diffuse radiation is assumed to come uniformly from the entire sky hemisphere. In reality it is brighter near the sun (circumsolar region) and near the horizon. A more accurate model (Perez) would require additional parameters.
  • ERA5 calibration resolution simplified — ERA5 has ~28 km resolution. The calibration gives the correct domain-average amplitude, but individual cells can exceed this by a factor related to their thermal factor (e.g. bare rock × 1.8).
  • No intra-day air temperature variation simplified — the model uses a single max_heat value for the whole day. In reality, air temperature rises through the afternoon, slightly reducing dT in the late afternoon relative to the morning at the same insolation level.

Effects not taken into account

  • Ambient wind not modelled — no synoptic or valley wind is included. Wind modifies thermal strength (shear, advection) and triggers blue thermals on windward slopes. It is the largest missing physical effect.
  • Soil moisture not modelled — wet soil splits incoming energy between sensible heat (raises air temperature) and latent heat (evaporation). A wet field after rain heats much less than dry bare ground. The ERA5 calibration partially absorbs the seasonal mean, but day-to-day variation is not captured.
  • No thermally-driven convergence or divergence not modelled — the surface flow arrows show anabatic (upslope) wind driven by the terrain gradient, but not the horizontal inflow that feeds the base of a thermal column, nor the lateral outflow at the thermal top. In reality, a strong thermal draws air from a radius of several hundred metres at the surface and spreads it aloft at the capping level. Modelling this correctly requires a divergence-free correction (solving a pressure Poisson equation), which is equivalent to a simplified Navier-Stokes step and would add significant complexity.
  • Clouds and cloud shadows not modelled — cumulus development, cloud shadows, and their suppression of ground heating are ignored.
  • Boundary layer dynamics not modelled — the mixing layer height, thermals overshooting the ELR ceiling, and entrainment are not simulated.
  • Subsidence and compensating sink not modelled — air that rises in a thermal must descend somewhere. This broad, weak sink is not represented.
  • Thermal triggering mechanism partially modelled — in reality thermals do not release continuously; heat accumulates under a surface inversion until a bubble detaches. The thermal release cooling (Phase 1) and heat advection (Phase 2) together approximate this: release drains excess heat from geometrically-favoured cells each step, while advection transports surrounding warmth into those cells to refuel them. The result is a continuous-release model with spatial heat redistribution. True episodic bubble release (accumulate → burst → reset cycle, O(minutes)) is not modelled.
  • Seasonal vegetation changes not modelled — land cover is static (2021 WorldCover). Spring snowmelt, leaf-out, and summer drought are not reflected.
  • Foehn, valley-exit jets, and local circulation not modelled — complex mountain meteorological phenomena driven by synoptic pressure gradients.
  • Turbulence and thermal structure not modelled — the plume profile is based on simple parcel buoyancy (see "Thermal plume profile" above), not a turbulent plume simulation. Entrainment, vortex ring structure, thermal pulsing, and the widening of real thermals with altitude due to mixing are all absent. The vertical speed shown is an upper-bound estimate.

Dual-resolution elevation strategy

The simulation and the visual terrain use different DEM sources. The SRTM 30 m grid is used for all physics computations: slope normals, shadow ray-casting, thermal tops, and orographic flow. A coarser grid keeps the computation and the exported JSON compact (15 time steps × 66 k cells).

The AWS Terrarium ~7 m tiles are fetched once and used only to build the visual terrain mesh in Three.js. At zoom level 14, the underlying source for France is IGN RGE Alti (LiDAR-derived), giving approximately 4–5× higher surface detail than SRTM. The tiles are RGB-encoded PNGs: elevation = R×256 + G + B/256 − 32768, delivered in Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) and reprojected to WGS84 (EPSG:4326) before use. The mesh elevation is exported once into the JSON alongside the simulation data.

Physics overlays (heatmap, thermal flow, etc.) are rendered on the coarser 30 m grid but are visually correct because they cover the same geographic extent and use depthWrite: false, floating just above the terrain surface.

Wind NS Simulation â€” Technical Documentation

Full 3D incompressible Navier-Stokes solver for wind flow over terrain with surface roughness

Overview

The Wind NS simulation solves the full 3D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations to model how a horizontal wind flows over real terrain. Unlike the 2D Shallow Water approach, this solver resolves the complete three-dimensional velocity field â€” including how wind accelerates over ridges, how it decelerates and recirculates in the lee, and how the logarithmic boundary layer develops above the surface. Surface roughness varies per land cover type, derived from the ESA WorldCover dataset, so forests slow the wind more than bare rock or water.

The simulation uses a Chorin projection method (fractional step) with semi-Lagrangian advection (stable for any CFL number) and a Jacobi pressure Poisson solver. It runs in the browser via a Web Worker on a 3D rectangular grid. Terrain cells are flagged as solid (no-flow); the wind profile is initialised with a logarithmic law anchored to each cell's land-cover roughness length.

Simplifications

  • Incompressibility: density is constant everywhere â€” no sound waves, no compressibility effects. Valid for wind speeds well below ~100 m/s. Eliminates the need for an energy equation.
  • Constant turbulent viscosity خ½: a single scalar viscosity is used rather than a spatially varying turbulence model (e.g. k-خµ or Smagorinsky). The Smagorinsky model is used in the Thermal NS simulation but not here â€” خ½ must be set manually.
  • Neutral atmosphere: no stratification, no buoyancy. The log profile assumes a neutral (adiabatic) atmospheric surface layer. Stable or unstable stratification would modify the profile (Monin-Obukhov similarity theory) but is not implemented.
  • Flat top boundary: the domain top is a horizontal plane with free-slip. This reflects vertical momentum back into the domain rather than allowing it to radiate upward â€” an artefact for strong updrafts reaching the top.
  • Jacobi pressure solver: converges slowly for large grids; many iterations may be needed to reach acceptable divergence residuals. SOR or a multigrid solver would converge faster but is not implemented.
  • Steady inflow: wind speed and direction are fixed in time. No gusts, no diurnal variation, no mesoscale dynamics.
  • No terrain-following coordinates: the grid is Cartesian; terrain is represented as blocked cells. Cells that straddle the exact terrain surface are either fully solid or fully fluid â€” no partial-cell treatment. This slightly over-estimates the terrain height by up to one dz.

Limitations

  • No thermal effects â€” no solar heating, no buoyancy, no convective thermals. For thermally driven vertical motion, use the Thermal NS simulation.
  • No atmospheric stratification â€” gravity waves, lee waves, and wave breaking (which cause severe turbulence at altitude) require a stratified atmosphere and are absent here.
  • No Coriolis force â€” negligible for domains < 50 km but formally absent.
  • No moisture â€” clouds, precipitation, and latent heat are not represented.
  • No synoptic forcing â€” large-scale pressure gradients, fronts, and mesoscale weather systems are not modelled. The inflow is a simple uniform log profile, not a realistic atmospheric wind profile with jets or wind shear.
  • Slow pressure convergence â€” the Jacobi solver requires many more iterations than SOR or multigrid to achieve low divergence residuals, especially on fine 3D grids. The flow may not be fully divergence-free within practical iteration counts.
  • No wall model â€” the log profile is enforced at inflow boundaries and at initialisation, but there is no dynamic wall model that adjusts the near-wall velocity at internal solid faces each step. Flow separation from steep terrain faces may be under-represented.
